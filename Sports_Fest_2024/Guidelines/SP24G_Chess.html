Chess is a strategy game played on a square board divided into 64 squares of alternating colors, typically black and white. Each player starts with sixteen pieces which is one king, one queen, two rooks, two knights, two bishops, and eight pawns.

Team/Player Composition

1 boy, 1 girl

Type of Tournament

Double Elimination

Game Mechanics

Rapid Chess (15 minutes per player)

Setup

  • Place the chess board so each player has a white square at their right-hand corner. Arrange the chess pieces on the board with each player having the same setup mirroring each other.

Objective

  • The goal is to checkmate your opponent’s king, meaning the king is in a position to be captured and cannot escape capture.

Movement of Pieces

  • Each type of piece moves in a specific way:
    • Kings move one square in any direction.
    • Queens move any number of squares in any direction: horizontally, vertically, or diagonally.
    • Rooks move any number of squares horizontally or vertically.
    • Knights move in an "L" shape: two squares in one direction and then one square perpendicular to that.
    • Bishops move any number of squares diagonally.
    • Pawns move forward one square, but they capture diagonally. On their first move, pawns have the option to move two squares forward.
  • Pieces cannot move through other pieces (though the knight is an exception) and cannot move to squares occupied by pieces of the same color.

Gameplay

  • Players will choose a side of a coin whether heads or tails to be flipped by the officiator of the game.
  • The flipped side will be the white side which is going to move first and then will alternate turns.
  • The game continues until one player is:
    • Checkmate/Stalemate - no legal moves for the king piece or other chess pieces to protect it.
    • Draw/Resign - declaring draw or resign will result in a win to his/her opponent.

Special moves

  • Castling
    • This move involves moving the king two squares towards a rook on its original square and then placing that rook on the square over which the king crossed. However, castling is only possible if neither the king nor the rook has moved previously, if there are no pieces between the king and rook, and if the king is not in check.
  • En Passant
    • If a pawn moves two squares forward from its starting position and lands adjacent to an opponent's pawn, the opponent has the option to capture the first pawn "en passant," as if it had only moved one square forward.

How to win

  • Strategize your moves until you checkmate or stalemate your opponent or until he/she loses time.
  • Declaration of draw or resignation from the opponent wins the game.

Do's and Don'ts

Do’s

  • Follow the clock move game policy.
  • Use only one hand in moving a piece and hitting the clock.

Don’ts

  • Don’t cheat in any way such as using a cheat sheet, inputting opponent’s moves into a chess bot, etc.
  • Do not say “check” if the opponent know they are in check. It may cause unnecessary conflict and/or distraction.
  • Don't distract your opponent intentionally. Respect their concentration and avoid any actions that could disrupt their thinking process.
  • Don't use electronic devices or reference materials during the game.
  • Don't make illegal moves or attempt to cheat in any way. Chess is a game of integrity, and fair play is essential.
  • Don't make unnecessary gestures or movements that could be interpreted as signaling your intentions or thoughts about the game.
  • Don't leave the playing area without permission, especially while it's your turn to move.
  • Don't forget to press the clock after making your move.
